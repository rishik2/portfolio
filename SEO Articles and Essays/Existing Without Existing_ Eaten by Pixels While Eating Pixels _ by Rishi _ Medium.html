Existing Without Existing: Eaten by Pixels While Eating Pixels

I looked around me, and people didn’t seem to exist in the present moment. They appeared headless, mummified, and entranced by the small gadget in their hands — a device blessed by marketing executives to seize their attention and even their very existence. A cursed smartphone.

I’m one of them, a drop in an ocean of many, whose life was consumed by the digital sea of pixels from early on, right since birth. First, it was the TV — a slow poison. They said it forged the nostalgia of my childhood, the cartoons I watched. Hats off to the creators of Tom and Jerry, Scooby-Doo, and Dexter. Then, restrictions were imposed, a concept known as “time limits.” Our parents ensured we didn’t overdose on pixels beyond our young minds’ capacity.

But with the arrival of adulthood, particularly in the late 2000s and between 2011 and 2015 (even earlier for the socially proactive bunch), the deluge of pixels became so overwhelming that not even parents and guardians could dam it. Heck, even they were engulfed by it.

There was MySpace, Orkut, Facebook, Twitter, Instagram, Snapchat, and then the portable TV known as YouTube. A never-ending stream of novelty was just a click away — everywhere, for everyone. It became so intertwined with our lives that resisting it led to becoming an outcast, a socio-psycho-deviant, an abnormality.

So, what’s there to bemoan in this march of technological progress? An obvious evolution, a step forward, something our adaptable minds would eventually acclimate to. This hyper-connectivity was deemed essential, a noble cause (as Mark Zuckerberg hinted in a podcast with Lex Friedman while discussing Meta’s inception and driving philosophy), or so we were told.

Even the spiritual leaders and evangelists, despite their typical inclination to resist most modern technological currents, are gradually shifting their stance on social media platforms and the allure of Play Store apps, notifications, and incessant reminders to stay online and connected — to them.

They consider it folly to blame technology for our perpetual state of distraction. Our anxieties, overstimulation, and brain fog, they argue, stem from within. The connection to pixels is coincidental; the truth is, your anxiety and attention deficit were always present.

Yoga through Facebook, followed by an organization hashtag. Meditate and upload a video. Do everything now on, around, and through the screen — while the screen replaces meals.

I am an advertisement and the advertised-to, and my life has fragmented as my sense of flow withers. Collectively, our sense of flow is disintegrating.

We struggle to engage with lengthy books beyond the simplicity of an Instagram caption. We can’t watch a movie like “The Godfather” without glancing at our phones numerous times. It’s fragments we reside in.

And these fragments are ruled by pixels, while the tangible world persists, beckoning for attention and care.

Our minds crave respite. Yet our moments of rest and activity are spent in the clutches of technology, its specters lingering even as we converse with loved ones or return home from a demanding day.

The wounds it inflicts are numerous, mental health included. We need resistance, our modest revolt — not to surrender ourselves to these data behemoths. If anything, disconnect for a while and find serenity.

We need it before pixels inundate us and everything significant in the world around us. Meanwhile, young Silicon Valley prodigies amass wealth at the cost of stealing our focus.

Deep work demands distancing ourselves from screen and smartphone addiction.
